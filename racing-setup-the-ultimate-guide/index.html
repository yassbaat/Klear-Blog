Changing the springs has a great effect on the setup, let’s see how it works:

Springs are rated in N/m (SI unit). But commercially,  you might find springs rated in kg/mm or lbs/inch, this indicates how much the spring deflects with an applied force. 

For example  , for a 7kg/mm ( 392 lbs/in )* spring , it takes 7kg to compress the spring one millimeter ( 392lbs to compress the spring one inch) .

 

*( the conversion rate between kg/mm to lbs/inch  is X56 )

 

To setup the vehicle, you should know the loads on the vehicle, it’s weight the aerodynamic loads etc.. 

First off, the suspension should support the car’s weight and keep a clearance off the ground, we can’t have the car frame dragging on the track of course.

Response:

  • Locking Rear Differential: Increased stability, potential understeer, reduced rear tire scrub.
  • Open Rear Differential: Increased agility, potential oversteer, increased rear tire scrub.
  • Balanced Approach: Adjust based on specific track conditions and driver preferences.

Detailed Explanation:

  1. Locking Rear Differential:
    • Traction: Locking the rear differential ensures both rear wheels rotate at the same speed. This can improve traction, especially during acceleration out of corners.
    • Understeer: By locking the rear differential, the car might tend to understeer. This happens because both rear wheels push the car forward equally, making it harder for the car to rotate or turn.
    • Rear Tire Scrub: Contrary to inducing oversteer, a locked differential can actually reduce the tendency for the rear tires to scrub against the pavement during cornering. Both wheels moving at the same speed can reduce differential slip, leading to a more stable rear end.
  2. Open Rear Differential:
    • Agility: An open differential allows each wheel to rotate at different speeds, which can help the car turn more easily. This often results in better agility and responsiveness during cornering.
    • Oversteer: With an open differential, there's a higher chance of oversteer because the inside wheel can lose traction more easily, causing the rear end to swing out.
    • Rear Tire Scrub: Open differentials can increase rear tire scrub during cornering, as the inside wheel might rotate slower or even momentarily stop, causing more wear and heat.

Practical Steps for Studying Differential Settings:

  • Data Collection: Gather telemetry data from your car under various differential settings. Focus on parameters like cornering speed, tire temperatures, and wheel slip.
  • Driver Feedback: Obtain feedback from drivers regarding the handling characteristics under different settings. Their input can be invaluable for understanding the subjective effects.
  • Track Testing: Conduct tests on different types of tracks. Different tracks can highlight the advantages or disadvantages of specific differential settings.
  • Simulation Tools: Use simulation software to model the effects of differential settings. This can help predict the outcomes without extensive on-track testing.

Recommendations:

  • Experiment with Settings: Try varying the degree of locking in the differential and observe the changes in handling. Partial locking might provide a balance between stability and agility.
  • Adjust for Conditions: Tailor the differential settings based on track conditions, weather, and the specific characteristics of the car and driver preferences.

In summary, while locking the rear differential can induce understeer due to the increased stability and reduced differential slip, it is essential to consider the overall impact on the car's handling dynamics and the specific needs of the driver and track conditions.

how does locking and opening the differential actually work? for the mechanics. say for the differential to be 50% open what should the mechanics change and how do they measure it?
 

Summary:

  • Differential Mechanics: Open, locking, and limited-slip differentials work differently.
  • Locking/Opening Mechanisms: Clutch packs, gears, electronic control.
  • Measuring Differential Lock: Use telemetry, specialized tools, and testing.

Detailed Explanation:

Differential Types:

  1. Open Differential:

    • Function: Allows each wheel to rotate independently, suitable for normal driving conditions.
    • Mechanics: Uses a set of gears (spider gears) that allow one wheel to rotate faster or slower than the other.

  2. Locked Differential:

    • Function: Forces both wheels on an axle to rotate at the same speed.
    • Mechanics: Achieved through mechanical locks or electronically controlled mechanisms. It eliminates the differential action, making both wheels move together.

  3. Limited-Slip Differential (LSD):

    • Function: Provides a compromise between open and locked differentials. It limits the difference in rotation speed between the two wheels.
    • Mechanics: Uses clutch packs, viscous fluid, or helical gears to achieve limited slip. The amount of slip is controlled by the preload on the clutches or the characteristics of the fluid/gear system.

Mechanisms for Locking and Opening the Differential:

  1. Clutch-Type LSD:

    • Components: Clutch packs, spring preload, friction plates.
    • Adjustment: Vary the preload on the clutch packs to change the locking percentage. More preload increases the locking effect.
    • Measurement: Measure the preload force and the friction coefficient to determine the locking percentage.

  2. Helical/Torsen LSD:

    • Components: Helical gears, worm gears.
    • Adjustment: Change the gear design or preload to adjust the torque bias ratio.
    • Measurement: Measure the torque bias ratio (the ratio of torque between the wheels) to determine the level of limited slip.

  3. Viscous LSD:

    • Components: Silicone-based fluid, perforated plates.
    • Adjustment: Change the viscosity of the fluid or the design of the plates.
    • Measurement: Measure the shear force generated by the fluid under different conditions.

  4. Electronic LSD:

    • Components: Electronic control unit (ECU), sensors, actuators.
    • Adjustment: ECU controls the amount of lock by adjusting actuators based on sensor input.
    • Measurement: Use telemetry data from sensors to determine the locking percentage in real-time.

Practical Steps for Adjusting Differential Lock:

  1. Identify the Differential Type:

    • Determine whether the car uses a clutch-type, helical, viscous, or electronic LSD.

  2. Adjust the Mechanism:

    • Clutch-Type: Adjust the preload on the clutch packs. This can be done by changing the springs or adjusting the tension.
    • Helical/Torsen: Modify the gear setup or preload.
    • Viscous: Change the viscosity of the fluid or the design of the plates.
    • Electronic: Adjust the ECU settings or actuators through software.

  3. Measure the Locking Percentage:

    • Telemetry Data: Use data from wheel speed sensors to analyze the difference in wheel speeds.
    • Specialized Tools: Use tools like a differential tester or a dyno to measure the torque bias ratio or locking force.
    • Track Testing: Conduct on-track testing to observe the handling characteristics and adjust based on performance and feedback.

Example Adjustment for a 50% Locking Differential:

  1. Clutch-Type LSD:

    • Adjustment: Increase the preload on the clutch packs to achieve 50% lock. This could involve tightening the preload springs or adding more friction plates.
    • Measurement: Use a differential tester to measure the preload force and ensure it corresponds to a 50% locking effect.

  2. Electronic LSD:

    • Adjustment: Program the ECU to achieve a 50% locking effect under specified conditions (e.g., during acceleration or cornering).
    • Measurement: Use telemetry data to monitor the wheel speeds and ensure the ECU is applying the correct amount of lock.

  3. Viscous LSD:

    • Adjustment: Use a fluid with a viscosity that achieves a 50% locking effect or modify the plate design to increase resistance.
    • Measurement: Measure the shear force generated by the fluid in a controlled environment to ensure it matches the desired locking percentage.

By understanding the mechanics and making precise adjustments, you can control the locking percentage of the differential to suit specific driving conditions and performance requirements.

  • Suggested Adjustments: Entry to mid-corner, exit from corners, high-speed straights.

Detailed Explanation:

Adjustable Differentials:

Modern race cars, especially high-performance vehicles like the Porsche 911 GT3, often come equipped with electronically controlled differentials (e-diffs) that allow the driver to adjust the differential settings on the fly. This capability enables drivers to optimize the car's handling for different sections of the track.

When to Adjust the Differential:

  1. Corner Entry to Mid-Corner:

    • Objective: Improve turn-in and mid-corner balance.
    • Suggested Setting: Increase the differential lock slightly (e.g., from 30% to 50%) to provide better stability and prevent oversteer as the car enters and negotiates the corner.
    • Driver Action: Adjust as you approach the braking zone and maintain through the apex.

  2. Corner Exit:

    • Objective: Maximize traction and acceleration.
    • Suggested Setting: Decrease the differential lock (e.g., from 50% to 30%) to allow the rear wheels to rotate more freely, reducing understeer and improving traction out of the corner.
    • Driver Action: Adjust as you start to accelerate out of the corner, ensuring the car gets maximum grip without excessive wheel spin.

  3. High-Speed Straights:

    • Objective: Ensure stability at high speeds.
    • Suggested Setting: Increase the differential lock to the maximum setting (e.g., up to 70-100%) to maintain stability and reduce differential slip, which can cause instability.
    • Driver Action: Adjust as you exit the last corner before the straight and maintain through the straight section.

Practical Steps for In-Lap Differential Adjustments:

  1. Pre-Race Setup:

    • Configure Controls: Ensure the car's steering wheel or control interface is set up to allow easy differential adjustments.
    • Preset Settings: Program preset differential settings for different sections of the track, if the car's electronics allow it.

  2. Driver Training:

    • Familiarization: Drivers should be thoroughly familiar with the controls and the impact of differential adjustments.
    • Practice Sessions: Use practice sessions to fine-tune the timing and magnitude of adjustments for different track sections.

  3. Telemetry Analysis:

    • Collect Data: Use telemetry to analyze the impact of differential adjustments on lap times and car handling.
    • Optimize Settings: Continuously refine the settings based on data and driver feedback to achieve the best balance.

3. With a locked diff, the inside wheel is forced to rotate faster than it naturally would, causing it to scrub and lose traction.
4. This loss of traction at the rear can cause the rear of the car to slide out, resulting in oversteer.
