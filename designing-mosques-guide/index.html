I. Design Basics & Overall Principles

1-Mosque Fundamental Design Principles:

I am not here to critique the mosque designs.

I am here to provide a perspective on how we can improve and implement design principles that can help elevate the experience of worshipers, make the environment more peaceful and calm and adhere to the principles of the prophet to avoid any kind of distraction when praying.

The principle that I want every Muslim to always have in mind, whether they are designing a mosque or just praying in it is: How can we improve the user experience in mosques for our fellow Muslims?

Solving the issues that nobody talks about is almost always the highest value. Solving a UX challenge for users, while the current solution in place is good enough.

Almost anyone can solve issues when they are apparent, the real challenge is to feel the pain of something that is not easily seen. As an example, Everyone can pick up something when they see it has fallen because it's easy physical work, our job as designers is to prevent that thing from falling in the first place, the designer sees what most people don't even notice.

In the following sections, we will go into the details of these principles:
- Enhancing the spiritual experience: 
- Provide a heightened spiritual experience, minimizing technology distractions inside the prayer hall which is one of the biggest issues of modern times to spirituality and focus.
- Minimizing physical distractions
-Balancing tradition with modern needs
-Making the essential frictionless, from wayfinding to finding all necessary amenities. Visiting the mosque should be an intuitive journey from the moment of Entering the mosque to leaving it.

 

Let's start from the outside.

Mosque Signs:

Every mosque should have a sign system. While people can ask each other and waste time looking for what they need, we could save so much time for worshipers by making a reliable, consistent sign system across all mosques in a city or country. That way we remove the guessing time and effort. I remember once praying in a city I've never been to (Rafraf), and the main mosque had an ablution area underground, I thought I was going to be early for the prayer but I kept looking for 5 minutes and couldn't figure out where it was, so I asked two people that gave me directions that weren't accurate because they assume that it is easy because they pray there every day. So I kept looking and I only eventually found it, when people started coming out after they have finished their Wudhu. I was late for the prayer.

Designing Mosque Signs Guide:

1- A sign of the mosque's name. There were times, that I didn't even notice that there is a mosque just because there was no sign.

Having signs helps the community facilitate having mosques as a geographical and spiritual reference.
Having signs with the mosque's name makes it easier for the community to use the mosque as a reference and meeting point. I.e there is a lecture in "Asala" mosque, or that shop is near that (insert mosque name) mosque. etc...

2- Overview of all amenities, this should be placed at the entry of the mosque. pointing out the directions of all the main a prayer hall in the first stair, ablution place, women's prayer hall etc...

3- Ablution sign, must be placed in the entry of the mosque, as well as near the main prayer hall, since most people can easily identify the prayer hall - because it's the main focus of the architecture.

4- The sign for the women prayer room should also be visible in the men's section.
Usually, when a woman wants to pray in a mosque, the men go first looking and asking if there's a women's prayer hall. So even if the women's designated prayer area access is far from the men's, a sign should be present showing the direction of the women's prayer area.

5- Provide any necessary information. Example, provide the phone number and name of responsible person for opening the door of the women's prayer hall, or the responsible for Tahfeeth.

II- Increasing Khushu' (Reverence) in The Prayer Hall:

1- Lights:

a- Having natural light inside the prayer hall is crucial. I don't prefer it when I go to some mosques at Thu'hr or Asr and find the lights turned on. I feel that I am at an office. Prayer should make one feel relaxed and having lights turned on at that time somewhat makes me tense. 

Windows:

Ceiling Windows " clerestory windows" are ideal for letting natural light in. They also create a beautiful, diffused light that can enhance the spiritual atmosphere.
Normal windows should be at a minimum of 1.25 meter above the ground (adjust according to mosque's environment).
Avoid Windows facing the Qibla to avoid distracting worshippers. If the parts that allow natural light are facing the Qibla, they should not be transparent, use Non-transparent materials like frosted glass or translucent panels. Or ideally they should be 2m+ above the ground. With a mechanism that allows for easy opening and closing for ventilation.

Lights should always, always, always be warm, please. Never have fluorescent bulbs. Night prayers are close to people's bedtimes, and having white light will not only disrupt some people's sleep but is not calming! 4500K+ .

Lights should be on the dim side. This instantly creates an atmosphere where people become calm. I noticed that some people start chatting and talking about worldly matters inside the mosque when they feel that they are in a social setting because of the intensity of the light.

According to research, dim lighting can reduce stress hormones and increase melatonin, promoting a more relaxed atmosphere. Although dim lighting is great, always make sure to allow for adequate lighting for reading, aiming for 150 lux.

I also suggest having an option to increase the light to boost attentiveness during Friday prayers and lectures.


2-Colors:

Avoid bright colors, especially inside the prayer hall. Colors should be neutral and do not distract the worshipers. That includes:
1-The carpets, especially the carpets! That's what the worshiper looks at when praying. There is a hadith in Bukhari and Muslim:

"The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) prayed in a black cloak that had markings, and he looked at its markings. When he finished praying, he said: "Take this cloak to Abu Jahm ibn Hudhayfah, and bring me his plain garment, for they distracted me just now when I was praying." Hishaam ibn 'Urwah narrated from his father, from 'Aa'ishah that the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said: "I was looking at its markings whilst I was praying and I was afraid that they would distract me."

The black cloak referred to (Khameesah) is a striped cloak made of silk or wool. The plain cloak referred to is a heavy cloak with no decoration on it.

-The walls shouldn't be unnecessarily decorated with colorful tiles or such.

  • The Mihrab - It's the central piece of the mosque, and should not have distractions for the imam. Why do you need to have expensive decorations? Is this from the Deen? Why are you inventing?
  • I would say, that I have one exception that can be accepted, which is using high-quality materials. Such as marble, high quality wood or even high quality carpets (only not decorated ones as we discussed earlier) and that is to reduce the cost of long-term maintenance. So always use high-quality materials if possible.

-The light fixtures/ chandeliers: Although these usually don't distract worshippers during prayer, as they are not in the field of view, chandeliers are expensive and do not usually make for an improvement of light quality.

-I suggest having spots pointing towards the Minbar and lecture desk, highlighting the Imam/Lecturer.

  • Although useful, I am actually against having a TV at the front of the prayer hall that displays the prayer times and events of the mosque. It makes it the center of attention, which obviously shouldn't be. I argue that the TV should be placed either outside the prayer hall in the patio, or at the back where it is not facing any worshippers that way you can see how much time is remaining for the Iqamah, events on the way out, and that's where you can engage in conversation such as planning to attend the lecture or when is the next prayer or the usual Iqamah times of the mosque. I argue that you shouldn't be waiting to get up to pray by the second, prayer should be a calm and stress free environment.

3-Furniture:

It is ideal to have custom made furniture. Such as seats, tables, desks, shoe racks and shelves. Making everything blend together, doing this not only provides the mosque with better quality longer lasting items, but also improves the experience, reduces the variation in color and makes everything consistent. Avoid plastic chairs, as those are usually known to break quickly making it unsafe for old people and sick people who cannot perform Sujud.

4- Accessibility of Quran copies:

Here are the criteria of where Quran copies should be placed: a- Should be placed in Open shelves b- Should be accessible yet high enough for all ages above 10 YO. c- Should never be placed in closed drawers and book lockers, that only increases friction for the readers and some people might not want to draw attention for themselves opening and closing the lockers especially if they are not familiar with the mosque, plus they take a big space and require more maintenance. So a big no for having book lockers.

 The number of Quran copies placed in the hall of prayer.

I think there is an exaggeration for the number of copies placed. Not to discourage anyone from buying copies of the Quran and placing them in the mosques! NO that's not the point! I eagerly want to push my brothers and sisters to do so, but, instead of placing a 150 copies in a prayer hall that has at maximum 100 people, why not make a study and figure out the optimal number +some margin. And the rest can be placed in other mosques, or gifted to people who need a copy.

I would say that the study should be done in 2 times, Jumuah prayers and Ramadan. That should cover the highest periods of reading for the Quran.

5- Acoustics

Acoustics (no need for microphones if the surface of the mosque is small, provides a better experience, only microphone auxiliary if the imam does have a high voice.)
- Make sure that the women's prayer area and/or other floors can hear the Imam in case the electricity goes off.

6- Women's Prayer Area

If on the same level, one way tinted mirror separation, is optimal, so that allows women in their prayer area to see into the men’s prayer hall without being seen themselves. 

This setup provides privacy for women while keeping them engaged and informed during prayers. Sujud tilawa, imam mistake, the gestures and body language of the imam at Jumuah Prayer etc...
The reflection of the mirror also makes the space in the men's prayer area feel bigger.

III- Amenities:

Accessibility:

Shoe racks shouldn't be too far from the pathway to the entrance of the prayer hall, failing to do so will encourage people to just drop their shoes in the nearest and most convenient place, which is the floor. (Even when there are enough shoe racks).

  • If there are any steps in the entrance, having ramps and handrails in the access of the mosque is mandatory, I can't count the number of mosques I've been to that forget this simple detail.

Re-defining ablution areas:

  1. Toilets should be outside the premises: In historic mosques you couldn't find toilets inside the premises of the mosque, only ablution areas, because there was no sewage system. I argue that this should be the same in our time.

Sheikh Abdul Aziz bin Baz and the members of the committee appear to favor prohibition. It is mentioned in the fatwas of the Permanent Committee:

The restroom must be separated so that it is outside the mosque, and there should be a wall between it and the mosque.

Ablution areas can be inside the mosque: فأباح ابن عباس رضي الله عنهما الوضوء من زمزم وهي في المسجد الحرام ونهى عن الاغتسال منها تنزيهاً للمسجد من التعري والاغتسال فيه من الجنابة

Toilets and ablution areas must be separate, ideally, we can place an ablution area inside and outside (near to the toilets but separated of) the mosque.

Having ablution areas separate from the toilets makes walking barefoot allowed to avoid "Najasa". The prayer area and ablution area should be connected with a slip-resistance material, Which is far better than having flip-flops which are limited in number and may cause skin irritations. Also, this allows for feet to dry, keeping carpets clean and avoid getting them wet which makes them smell bad. And reduce the reliance of having paper towel to dry.

New Concept: Showers in Ablution Areas:

Since toilets are not inside the mosque they should include at least one shower, so that travelers that don't have a place to stay (or homeless people) can shower from Janabah or to clean themselves before entering the mosque. These showers should have a waterproof closet to put all of one's belongings. The number of showers should not be excessive, even in big mosques, so that the mosque does not become a destination for people to take showers. Just enough to serve the purpose.

Practical standard: estimate the number of faucets needed.

  • Ablution Estimation:

    • Average Ablution Time: 2 minutes.
    • Average Iqamah Time: 10 minutes (adjust based on your mosque's timings).
    • People Needing Wudu:
      • 15% in residential areas.
      • Up to 40% in commercial and non-residential areas.
      • Use 25% for mixed areas.

  • Formula:

    Number of Toilets Required= ((Maximum Number of Worshippers)×(Wudu Percentage/100)×(AIT+Allowance) )/ ( AAT×CC )
​

    Where:

    • AIT: Average Iqamah Time ( in min )
    • Allowance: Waiting allowance (in min)
    • AAT: Average Ablution Time 
    • CC: Conversion Constant

    Waiting Allowance:

    • Minimum: 
      −AIT+2
      for lowest estimated wait time
    • Maximum: 6 for the highest allowance to reduce costs

  • Example Calculation:

    Number of Toilets= 
(1000×0.15) / (10+ (5/2)) = 150/12.5 =12

  • Optimum and Minimum Number of Toilets:

    • Optimum Number:Number of faucets / 2.
    • Minimum Number:Number of faucets / 3.

Mosque Example:

    • Number of Worshippers: 800
    • Number of Faucets: 20
    • Number of Toilets: 6

  • Usage Patterns:

    • Regular Days: 150 worshippers.
    • Jumah Prayer: Full capacity.

  • Notes:

    • Regular Days and Friday Prayers: The number of faucets is adequate.
    • Waiting Times for Toilets: Acceptable waiting times on regular days but unacceptable during Friday prayers.

      Using the formula above, and plugging values Targeting Minimum Cost:

      • Required Minimum Number of Faucets: 25
      • Minimum Number of Toilets: 25/3 = 8.3 rounded to 9. so this example doesn't even meet the minimum requirement of number of toilets.
      • Optimum Number of Toilets:25/2 = 12.5 rounded to 12. So for the above example, 6 toilets are missing for optimum waiting times.

Ablution and toilet areas checklist:

  • Every Ablution area must have a mirror, a place to put small items (glasses, keys etc...), clothes hanger, soap dispenser.
  • Ensure proper drainage design, especially near the faucets. Standing in a puddle while you're trying to get clean it just defeats the purpose, doesn't it? 
  • Design the floor with adequate slope towards drains to prevent water pooling around faucets. 
  • Install low-flow faucets
  • dual-flush toilets, and other water-saving devices to minimize water use.
  • No need for ablution partitions in mosques; they're an unnecessary expense. Without partitions, people can perform ablution closer together, enhancing the sense of community and allowing adults to assist the young for example.
  • Ensure enough space and easy access in ablution areas for people with special needs.

 

IV. Sustainability:

Building Materials:

Using local materials not only improves the aesthetics of the mosque, as earthy, natural materials are often perceived as more calming and authentic but may, cost less, last longer and reduce environmental impact compared to synthetic materials.

Financial Sustainability:

Mosques use X things that are the main expenses:
- Electricity for lighting, heating and cooling
-Water for ablution and cleaning
- Salaries for services such as cleaning and maintenance.

All of which can be made sustainable using these three things:
- Natural ways of lighting, heating, and cooling + natural power generation.

- Reuse ablution water for watering plants and flushing toilets. This reduces freshwater consumption and maintain the mosque's trees, which helps in cooling the surroundings.



If available, digging a well can be a good idea to ensure that water is always available and can help lower the water consumption cost in the long run.

Contributive maintenance system:

Maintaining and cleaning mosques should be the responsibility of all worshipers. I am pretty sure that all worshipers would be willing to contribute, however most people fear that they are getting involved in the responsibilities of the crew of the mosque.

A Paper can be placed with periodic to-dos in adequate places. Mopping the ablution area, vacuum cleaning the carpet, etc...

Everyone can contribute.

 

Maintenance:

Finally, I cannot stress the importance of regular maintenance. Mosques like every public space need thorough and regular maintenance. Failing to do so from the beginning will only lead to heightened expenses and a less enjoyable experience.
Here are some ways mosques can be financially sustainable:

  • If space allows it, having retail stores that can be rented out can bring in stable revenue.
    This is perfectly allowed according to scholars:
    It is permissible to have shops beneath the mosque that are rented out to support the mosque's needs.
    Just make sure that these stores are clearly separated from the premises of the mosque.

  • Having a box of donation is great, which is better is having specific boxes by category that worshippers can put money, (box for tissue paper, paper for electricity and water contribution, box for re-painting the mosque, for bulbs etc...)
