"The Maker's Contract".

The art of getting (a lot) things done.

 

"The Maker's Contract" is inspired by "The Cult of Done Manifesto". I discovered this piece of writing not too long ago, and I feel that it is the best thing that I have read that resonates with someone who is a polymath like me. So here's my version of it.

  • There are three states for a maker:
    1- Not knowing
    2- Working on the project
    3- Publishing/Launching it.

If you do not know something, you should research it. If you have researched it for a few hours, then in theory, you should be knowledgeable about that subject more than someone who has never encountered it before. You can, in fact, share what you know with them; that counts in my book as knowing it. So you should move on to the action phase; you should work on that project without getting distracted by learning. Because learning snaps you out of the maker state. Researching more won't make your work perfect because: Everything is a draft.


  • Focus on the first 15 minutes. What can I make in the following 15 minutes? Start there. Don't think about the end yet.
    Try to publish your work in the fewest number of 15-minute intervals possible.

  • Hate perfection. It's tedious and won't move the needle. It stops you from making and sharing stuff. Perfection is the enemy.

  • As a human, you are not perfect. If you want to be perfect, never do anything.

  • If you fail, that counts as done. Only in this case, you can go back to the learning phase. And maybe you discover that this project isn't as fun or interesting as you thought. Even then, the advantage of failing is you won't say: "what if?"

  • Have a place to publish your work. That way you know it is done. That way you stop your perfectionism and the countless unpublished work.
    Unpublished work = Unfinished work.
    Published work = Made work.

 

To be a maker, you have to think differently. You cannot afford to wander around too much when executing your project. You have to have a certain can-do attitude. It's necessary to wonder; however, once you start making, you have to engage in what you do and stop learning.
